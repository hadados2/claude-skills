# Behavioral Psychology Engine

## Critical Guidelines

You MUST ground every insight in a named psychological mechanism, not generic marketing intuition.
You MUST identify both the barrier (what stops action) and the motivator (what drives it) before recommending anything.
You MUST connect psychological insights directly to observable product, copy, or UX elements.

## Core Frameworks

### 1. BJ Fogg Behavior Model
Behavior = Motivation × Ability × Prompt (B = MAP)

- **Motivation axes:** Pleasure/Pain, Hope/Fear, Social Acceptance/Rejection
- **Ability:** time, money, physical effort, cognitive effort, social deviance, non-routine
- **Prompt:** trigger arrives when motivation and ability are both high enough

**Conversion failure diagnosis:**
- High motivation, low ability → simplify the action
- High ability, low motivation → amplify the stakes or desire
- Both present, no prompt → add timely, contextual trigger

### 2. Jobs-to-Be-Done (JTBD)
Users "hire" products to make progress in their life. The real job is rarely the surface request.

- **Functional job:** the practical task (e.g., "send money")
- **Emotional job:** how they want to feel (e.g., "feel in control of finances")
- **Social job:** how they want to be perceived (e.g., "look financially responsible")

Surface the emotional and social jobs — those drive conversion more than functional benefits.

### 3. Loss Aversion & Prospect Theory
Losses feel ~2x more painful than equivalent gains feel good (Kahneman & Tversky).

- Frame value as preventing loss, not just gaining benefit
- Identify what the user is already losing by NOT acting
- Status quo bias: inaction feels "safe" — reframe inaction as the risky choice

### 4. Cialdini's Persuasion Principles
| Principle | Mechanism | Conversion Application |
|---|---|---|
| Reciprocity | Give first, receive later | Free value before ask |
| Commitment | Small yes leads to bigger yes | Progressive engagement |
| Social Proof | Others like me did this | Testimonials, usage stats |
| Authority | Expert validation | Credentials, press, data |
| Scarcity | Limited availability raises value | Deadlines, limited spots |
| Liking | People buy from people they like | Relatable brand voice |
| Unity | In-group identity | "For people like you" |

### 5. Fear, Uncertainty, Doubt (FUD) vs. Hope
- **FUD converts** when the threat feels real and proximate (not abstract)
- **Hope converts** when the desired outcome feels achievable (not aspirational fantasy)
- Diagnose: is the audience stuck in fear paralysis, or hope skepticism?

### 6. Cognitive Load & Decision Fatigue
- Every choice costs mental energy — reduce options, simplify paths
- Analysis paralysis kills conversion: present one clear recommended action
- The paradox of choice: more options = lower satisfaction + lower conversion

## Behavior Analysis Process

**Step 1 — Surface the real job**
Ask: What progress is the user trying to make? What does success look and feel like for them?

**Step 2 — Map the emotional arc**
Before state (frustrated, anxious, hopeful?) → Desired after state (confident, relieved, proud?)

**Step 3 — Identify the core fear**
What is the user afraid will happen if they act? What do they stand to lose (money, time, status, identity)?

**Step 4 — Identify the core desire**
What outcome do they desperately want? What would make them feel like they "won"?

**Step 5 — Diagnose the conversion barrier**
Use B = MAP:
- Is motivation low? → Strengthen emotional stakes
- Is ability low? → Remove friction
- Is prompt missing or poorly timed? → Fix the trigger

**Step 6 — Apply the right framework**
Match the psychological lever to the barrier identified.

## Conversion Diagnosis Template

When asked why users convert or don't, structure the analysis as:

```
AUDIENCE STATE
- Who they are: [identity, context, awareness level]
- Before state: [emotional state before encountering product]
- Desired after state: [what they want to feel/achieve]

PSYCHOLOGICAL DRIVERS
- Core fear: [what they're afraid of]
- Core desire: [what they desperately want]
- Social pressure: [how others influence the decision]

CONVERSION ANALYSIS
- Motivation level: [high/medium/low + why]
- Ability/friction: [what makes acting hard]
- Prompt quality: [is trigger well-timed and relevant]

BARRIER DIAGNOSIS
- Primary blocker: [the #1 reason they don't act]
- Secondary blockers: [other friction points]

RECOMMENDATIONS
- Psychological lever to pull: [named mechanism]
- Specific change: [concrete copy, UX, or positioning change]
- Expected mechanism: [why this should work]
```

## Common Patterns

**"Looks good but doesn't convert"**
→ Usually: functional benefits communicated, emotional/social jobs ignored. Reframe around identity and feeling, not features.

**"High traffic, low signups"**
→ Usually: motivation present, ability broken. Audit cognitive load, form friction, and trust signals at the decision moment.

**"Converts but churns"**
→ Usually: promised emotional outcome not delivered. The "after state" expectation was set incorrectly.

**"Ad clicks but no purchases"**
→ Usually: ad triggers one emotional job, landing page sells a different one. Mismatched emotional arc.

**"Users say they love it but don't buy"**
→ Usually: liking ≠ hiring. The product isn't solving a high-stakes job. Find the friction between appreciation and necessity.

## Emotional Trigger Vocabulary

**Fear triggers:** losing status, wasting money, making the wrong choice, being judged, missing out, falling behind competitors, looking foolish

**Desire triggers:** being seen as successful/smart/innovative, saving time for what matters, certainty and control, belonging to a winning group, transformation (before → after)

**Identity triggers:** "people like me do X", professional identity, values alignment, aspirational self-image
